Redlining in Boston

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Is there a geographic pattern to redlining in the Greater Boston area? - The background reading states that the HOLC created a uniform system of neighborhood grades that color-coded segments of major cities (Catherine Elton, 2020). The text highlights that sections of Roxbury were rated "hazardous" despite having good transportation and job proximity, while neighborhoods in suburbs like Milton were rated "still desirable". Investigating the spatial distribution will help visualize if the government's policy systematically targeted dense urban centers while favoring the "suburban home-building spree" mentioned in the text. From previous classes I have taken like 'The Ghetto', I have seen it is common to segregate minority populations into under-maintained, high density city centers while majorities move out to suburbs, practically segregating by flight.
  2. I am interested in seeing if these low grades were geographically tied to industrial sites, which might have lowered the perceived "security" of the investment for the HOLC. Also, location-wise it made sense to me that low-income and minority populations might work in more industrial areas rather than commercial so there might be some relationship with proximity to these areas.
  3. By investigating the city and grade fields in the dataset, I can explore if the "Desirable" ratings were disproportionately allocated to the exclusionary suburbs mentioned as "90 percent white" in the background reading.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

This analysis demonstrates that redlining in Greater Boston was not merely a set of disconnected ratings but a systematic geographic project that fundamentally penalized urban density and industrial proximity. A primary lesson learned is the spatial penalty enforced by the HOLC: neighborhoods in the urban core, such as Boston, Chelsea, and Everett, were disproportionately redlined, with Boston seeing 30.7% of its areas graded as "Hazardous". In contrast, suburban municipalities like Winchester were insulated from these designations, with 0% of their area receiving a 'C' or 'D' grade. This geographic gradient effectively subsidized the suburban home-building spree for white populations while systematically denying investment to minority and low-income groups anchored in the city centers. Furthermore, the study reveals a definitive spatial correlation between industrial zoning and residential disinvestment. The proximity analysis confirms that Grade D neighborhoods were often physically engulfed by industrial sites, maintaining a median distance of 0.0 miles from factories, compared to an insulation distance of nearly 2 miles for "Best" (A) rated areas. While some smaller industrial hubs like Salem and Lynn showed more varied social fabrics where industrial and high-grade residential zones overlapped, the urban core exhibited a rigid adjacency between labor, industry, and low HOLC grades. These findings underscore how planners utilized land-use patterns to codify racial and economic segregation, creating a process of urban deterioration that remains visible in Boston’s landscape today.
